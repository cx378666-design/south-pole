Climate of Antarctica

Explore the extreme cold, massive ice sheets, and the impact of climate change in the southernmost continent.

Antarctica Climate

Extreme cold and icy landscapes with fascinating scientific studies.

Extreme Temperatures

Antarctica is the coldest continent on Earth. Winter temperatures can drop below -80°C, while summer along the coast may rise to just -2°C.

Lowest recorded temperature: -89.2°C at Vostok Station (1983)

Average coastal summer temperature: -2°C to 8°C

Ice Sheets & Glaciers

Antarctica is covered by a massive ice sheet, holding about 60% of the world’s freshwater. The ice sheet averages 1.9 km thick, and glaciers flow slowly towards the ocean.

Ross Ice Shelf

Largest ice shelf in Antarctica, about the size of France.

Larsen Ice Shelf

Known for dramatic collapse events due to warming climate.

Global Warming & Impact

Rising global temperatures are causing ice sheets to melt and sea levels to rise. Antarctica is a critical indicator of climate change.

  • Antarctic ice loss rate: ~252 billion tons per year (2010–2019)
  • Contributes to global sea level rise: ~0.7 mm per year
  • Changing wildlife patterns due to melting ice

Weather Phenomena

Antarctica experiences katabatic winds, blizzards, and prolonged polar night or day. These extreme conditions shape the ecosystem and research operations.

Scientific Research

Antarctica is a hub for climate research. Scientists monitor ice cores, atmospheric CO2, and temperature trends to understand Earth's changing climate.

Research Stations

Over 70 stations operate worldwide, gathering data year-round.

Ice Core Studies

Ice cores reveal historical climate data spanning hundreds of thousands of years.
