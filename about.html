About Antarctica

Explore the mysteries of the southernmost continent — a place of wonder, research, and survival.

History of Exploration

Antarctica remained undiscovered until the early 19th century. The first confirmed sighting occurred in 1820, and since then, it has become a symbol of scientific cooperation and adventure.

James Cook

Captain Cook was one of the first explorers to venture near the Antarctic Circle, paving the way for future expeditions.

Ernest Shackleton

His heroic expeditions showcased human endurance against the harshest of climates.

Roald Amundsen

In 1911, Amundsen became the first person to reach the South Pole — a milestone in human exploration.

Geography & Environment

Antarctica is covered by an ice sheet that holds 60% of Earth's freshwater. Beneath the ice lie mountains, valleys, and subglacial lakes. The continent has no permanent human population — only scientists and researchers live here temporarily.

Scientific Importance

Antarctica plays a vital role in understanding our planet’s climate. Research stations study meteorology, glaciology, and wildlife to help predict global environmental changes.

  • Over 70 research stations across the continent
  • Key for global climate monitoring
  • Provides valuable satellite data for Earth studies
